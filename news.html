Success Story November 2009

Armed Gang Foiled in Johannesburg Jewelry Store

Shortly after opening time on a Wednesday morning in Johannesburg, six armed criminals burst into a jewelry store in a shopping mall. They took hold of the security guards and made straight for the safe.

The store had installed a Smoke Screen unit just one month earlier. A staff member hit the activation button. The room filled with dense fog in seconds.

The thieves tried to reach the safe. They couldn't see it. They couldn't see anything. Disoriented and unable to navigate by feel, they stumbled around the concealed merchandise and found nothing.

Twenty-three seconds after entering the store, they fled. Empty-handed. Nobody in the store was hurt. Nothing was taken. The only cost to the business was the activation fluid, which refills in minutes.

Industry News October 2010

Security Fog Gets an International Standard

The IEC (International Electrotechnical Commission) accepted the European Standard for Security Fog Devices, EN 50131-8, to be developed into a full International Standard. The draft designation is IEC 62642-8.

The IEC publishes manufacturing and performance standards for electrical and electronic technologies worldwide. Its guidelines exist so that insurers, alarm companies, and end customers can evaluate products against a fixed, independent benchmark rather than relying on supplier claims alone.

For security fog, the standard covers everything from electrical safety and performance to deployment density, residue, and alarm panel integration. Smoke Screen products are built and tested against these requirements. When comparing fog security providers, ask any supplier whether their equipment meets IEC 62642-8. If they can't answer clearly, that tells you something.

Warehouse May 2011

Three Raiders, a 7-Ton Truck, and Zero Loot

On May 12th 2011, shortly after midnight, an organized gang targeted the Euronics warehouse in Frankfurt, Germany. This was not a casual break-in. Three experienced thieves scaled the roof while a getaway driver waited below in a 7-ton truck, ready for a large haul.

The warehouse held over €4 million in mobile phones, flat-screen TVs, and electronics. It was also protected by a Safetec alarm system integrated with a Smoke Screen security fog unit.

The thieves axed open a skylight and lowered a folding ladder. That triggered the alarm and the Smoke Screen fired. The entire warehouse filled with dense fog in seconds.

Looking down from the skylight, the gang saw nothing but white. Their intended loot had disappeared. They retreated from the roof and fled. The whole attempt lasted less than 20 seconds.

A stock check the next morning confirmed nothing was taken. The only damage: one broken skylight. Smoke Screen had protected millions of dollars in stock in a single activation.

Retail February 2012

Four Sledgehammers, One Jewelry Store, Ten Seconds

On January 30th 2012, four masked men armed with sledgehammers forced their way into Ram Jewelers, a prominent jewelry store in Leicester, England, while it was still open for business.

They smashed through the front door and headed straight for the glass display cabinets. Owner Mahul Visram moved staff and customers to the back of the store, then activated the Smoke Screen system.

All four attackers fled in a waiting car. Despite the violence of the entry and the obvious plan to grab gold, they left with only a fraction of what they came for. The fog had covered the merchandise before they could reach it.

No staff or customers were hurt. The system did exactly what it was installed to do: shield the goods, protect the people, end the robbery.

Brand History November 2012

Security Fog: Where It Started

Smoke Screen installed the world's first security fog system in gold bullion vaults in the UK in the early 1970s. The concept was straightforward: if you can't see the vault, you can't rob it. It worked then, and it still works now.

The technology became commercially available in April 1991. In the decades since, it has gone through multiple generations of improvements: smaller units, faster deployment, more precise fluid chemistry, and smarter alarm integrations.

Today, Smoke Screen products meet IEC 62642-8 and EN 50131-8 international standards, are approved by major insurers, and are deployed in over 50 countries. The fog has been verified residue-free by the UK Atomic Energy Authority and independently confirmed by Molecular Profile Services.

School Security January 2013

Can Security Fog Protect Schools?

In January 2013, following the Sandy Hook tragedy, Smoke Screen US president Eric Howe proposed using security fog in American schools. The idea: a unit in each classroom, a handheld remote for the teacher, and the ability to fill the room with dense, non-toxic fog within seconds of a lockdown.

"We would like to put it into classrooms and have a single machine in each classroom with a teacher with a remote control, so with the press of a button, within seconds the entire classroom is filled," Howe told Fox 7 Austin. "In the event of a lockdown situation, the teacher would put the children in the safest part of the classroom, sit them down, press the button."

The logic is the same as in any commercial deployment: a threat that cannot see its target is far less likely to act, and far more likely to leave. Smoke Screen has since been installed in educational facilities across multiple countries.

Technology July 2013

No Residue. Independently Confirmed.

The most common concern business owners raise about security fog is whether it will damage their stock, equipment, or surfaces. For Smoke Screen, the answer is no, and that is backed by independent science, not just a product claim.

Concept Smoke Screen generators produce a uniform particle size of 0.2 microns, verified by the Atomic Energy Authority's Aerosols Division. The industry average is around 2 microns. At 0.2 microns, Smoke Screen particles are 1,000 times smaller by volume, which means the fog settles at roughly 9mm per hour and leaves no residue on any surface it contacts.

An independent test conducted by Dr. Shen of Molecular Profile Services used Atomic Force Microscopy on surfaces exposed to Smoke Screen deployment at five to ten times normal operating concentration. Conclusion: "I am pleased to say that these show that there are no signs of contamination that could be associated with your smoke particles."

"The importance of droplet size is absolutely paramount when choosing a system to protect your business," noted Eric Howe, President of Concept Smoke Screen US.
